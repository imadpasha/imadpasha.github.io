A Search for Lensed AGN in the CLASH Cluster Sample






















Gravitational lensing is a powerful tool for probing high redshift galaxies at and beyond z=2, when the universe's star formation rate peaked. Massive clusters of galaxies both magnify and increase the spatial resolution of objects lying behind them, allowing for the detection and characterization of sources that would have been too faint for current instrumentation (see Kneib and Natarajan 2011 for a recent review of the field). Typically, surveys to find lensing candidates are carried out at optical and near-infrared wavelengths. More recently, one massive cluster MACS J0717.5+3745 was studied at radio and X-ray wavelengths (van Weeren et al. 2016).

X-ray studies in particular are difficult to carry out due to the high levels of X-ray continuum from the intracluster medium (ICM), which increases the background signal even at hard bands (massive clusters tend to be X-ray hot (~6-12keV)). However, with deep enough imaging and background subtraction, it may be possible to find a handful of highly magnified X-ray sources behind massive clusters. These sources would almost certainly be active galactic nuclei (AGN), yielding a sample of AGN at high redshift for further study. To date, only four X-ray sources behind clusters have ever been found, two in MACS J0717.5+3745 (van Weeren et al. 2016) and two in Abell 370 (Bautz et al. 2000). Before any systematic work can be done to understand the properties of these AGN at high redshift, more of them have to be discovered and characterized.

Because the chances of finding well-aligned AGN via gravitational lensing is lower than that for normal galaxies (due to their rarity), a large sample of clusters is required if a study of lensed X-ray sources is to be carried out. CLASH (Cluster Lensing And Supernova survey with Hubble) is one such sample (see Postman et al. 2012 for details). The CLASH survey observed twenty-five massive clusters between z=0.187 and z=0.89 over the course of 524 orbits of HST with sixteen broadband filters, allowing for fairly precise photometric redshifts to be obtained. CLASH is a survey well suited to carrying out an X-Ray source search because (1) photometric redshifts are provided for all objects in the field, (2) magnification maps of each cluster region are provided based on the models of Zitrin et al. 2011, (3) CLASH contains several very high mass clusters, which are the best candidates for finding highly magnified objects (MACS J0717, for example, is included in the sample).
